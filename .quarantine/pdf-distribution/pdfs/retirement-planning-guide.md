## The 4% Rule: A Useful Starting Point (But Not the Final Answer)

You've probably heard of the **4% rule**. It says: In your first year of retirement, withdraw 4% of your portfolio, then adjust that dollar amount for inflation each year. If you follow this, your money should last at least 30 years.

**Example:** If you need $50,000 per year from your investments (after Social Security or pensions), you'd need:

**$50,000 ÷ 0.04 = $1,250,000**

That's your target nest egg—assuming the 4% rule holds. But here's the catch: The 4% rule was based on historical U.S. stock and bond returns. If you retire early (before 65), have high healthcare costs, or face a prolonged bear market, you may need to withdraw less—or save more.

## The Real Number: A 4-Step Process to Calculate Your Target

Forget guessing. Here's a repeatable process to find *your* number.

### Step 1: Estimate Your Annual Retirement Expenses

Most people underestimate this. Start with your current annual spending, then adjust:

- **Add** healthcare costs (Medicare premiums, dental, long-term care)
- **Add** travel, hobbies, or gifts you plan to increase
- **Subtract** work-related costs (commuting, dry cleaning, lunches out)
- **Subtract** mortgage payments if you'll own your home free and clear

**Rule of thumb:** Plan for 70–80% of your pre-retirement income. But if you want to travel extensively or have expensive hobbies, it could be 100% or more.

### Step 2: Account for Social Security & Other Income

Log into your Social Security account at ssa.gov to see your estimated benefit at different claiming ages (62, 65, 67, 70). Delaying to age 70 increases your monthly check by 8% per year.

Also factor in:
- Pensions (if you have one)
- Rental income
- Part-time work (many retirees work 10–20 hours per week)

**Net annual need = Total expenses – Other income**

This net amount is what your investment portfolio must provide.

### Step 3: Choose a Withdrawal Rate

| Withdrawal Rate | Portfolio Needed for $40,000/year | Risk Level |
|----------------|----------------------------------|------------|
| 3% (conservative) | $1,333,333 | Very low |
| 4% (standard) | $1,000,000 | Low |
| 5% (aggressive) | $800,000 | Moderate |
| 6%+ (risky) | $666,667 | High |

**Recommendation:** Use 3.5% to 4% for a traditional retirement (age 65+). Use 3% if retiring early (before 55).

### Step 4: Factor in Inflation

Inflation is the silent portfolio killer. At 3% average inflation, $50,000 today will be worth about $67,000 in 10 years and $90,000 in 20 years. Your retirement number must grow with inflation.

## How to Close the Gap: Saving and Investing

Once you know your target, you need a plan to get there.

### The Power of Compound Interest

If you're 30 years old with $50,000 saved and you add $10,000 per year, earning 7% annually, you'd have approximately **$1.4 million by age 65**. That's the magic of compounding—your money earns money on the money it already earned.

### Key Rules for Closing the Gap

1. **Maximize tax-advantaged accounts:** 401(k), IRA, HSA, Roth IRA.
2. **Invest broadly:** Low-cost index funds (S&P 500, total bond market).
3. **Increase savings rate by 1% per year** or whenever you get a raise.

## Common Pitfalls That Derail Retirement Plans

### 1. Ignoring Healthcare Costs
A 65-year-old couple retiring today will spend an estimated $300,000+ on healthcare in retirement (Fidelity estimate). Don't forget Medicare premiums, deductibles, and long-term care insurance.

### 2. Underestimating Longevity
One in three 65-year-olds today will live past age 90. Plan for at least 30 years of retirement. If you retire at 55, plan for 40 years.

### 3. Overestimating Investment Returns
Don't assume 10% annual returns. A more realistic long-term average for a balanced portfolio (60% stocks / 40% bonds) is 6–7% before inflation, or 4–5% after inflation.
