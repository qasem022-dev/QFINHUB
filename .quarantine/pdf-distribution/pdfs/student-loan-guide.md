# Student Loan Repayment: Choose the Right Plan

> Brought to you by QFINHUB — Free Financial Calculators at qfinhub.com

Choosing the right student loan repayment plan is one of the most consequential financial decisions you’ll make after graduation. With the average student loan borrower in the U.S. carrying over $37,000 in debt, selecting a plan that aligns with your income, career trajectory, and long-term financial goals can save you thousands of dollars—or cost you dearly if you choose poorly.

This guide walks you through the major repayment options, their trade-offs, and how to use QFINHUB’s free calculators to model your unique situation.

## Understanding Your Loan Types First

Before you can choose a repayment plan, you need to know what kind of loans you have. Federal student loans (Direct Subsidized, Direct Unsubsidized, PLUS, and Perkins loans) offer flexible repayment options that private loans do not.

**Key distinction:** Only federal loans qualify for income-driven repayment plans, loan forgiveness programs, and deferment/forbearance options. Private loans are governed by the terms of your lender contract.

**Action step:** Log into the National Student Loan Data System (NSLDS) at studentaid.gov to see all your federal loans, their interest rates, and current servicers.

## The Four Main Repayment Plan Categories

### 1. Standard Repayment Plan
- **Term:** 10 years (up to 30 years for consolidated loans)
- **Payment:** Fixed monthly amount
- **Best for:** Borrowers with stable income who want to minimize total interest paid
- **Trade-off:** Highest monthly payment, but you’ll pay the least interest overall

### 2. Graduated Repayment Plan
- **Term:** 10 years
- **Payment:** Starts low, increases every two years
- **Best for:** Early-career professionals expecting significant income growth
- **Trade-off:** You’ll pay more total interest than the standard plan because you’re paying less principal early on

### 3. Extended Repayment Plan
- **Term:** Up to 25 years
- **Payment:** Fixed or graduated
- **Best for:** Borrowers with large loan balances (over $30,000 in Direct Loans) who need lower monthly payments
- **Trade-off:** Dramatically higher total interest costs

### 4. Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) Plans
These include IBR, PAYE, REPAYE/SAVE, and ICR plans. Payments are calculated as a percentage (10%–20%) of your discretionary income.

- **Term:** 20–25 years (remaining balance forgiven)
- **Payment:** Can be as low as $0 if income is low
- **Best for:** Borrowers with high debt relative to income, those in public service, or those pursuing PSLF
- **Trade-off:** Tax on forgiven balance (unless under PSLF), and significantly more interest paid over time

## Comparing Key Plans Side-by-Side

| Plan | Term | Monthly Payment | Total Interest (on $35k at 5%) | Forgiveness? |
|------|------|----------------|-------------------------------|--------------|
| Standard | 10 years | ~$371 | ~$9,550 | No |
| Graduated | 10 years | $215–$558 | ~$11,200 | No |
| Extended (fixed) | 25 years | ~$205 | ~$26,400 | No |
| SAVE (IDR) | 20–25 years | 10% of discretionary income | Varies widely | Yes, taxable |

**Note:** The SAVE plan (formerly REPAYE) offers the most generous terms for undergraduate borrowers, including a higher income exemption and interest subsidy that prevents your balance from growing if your payment doesn’t cover accrued interest.

## How to Choose: A Decision Framework

### Step 1: Calculate your debt-to-income ratio
Divide your total student loan balance by your annual gross income. Use QFINHUB’s **Loan Calculator** at qfinhub.com/calculators/loan to model different scenarios.

- **Ratio < 1.0:** You can likely afford the Standard plan and save on interest.
- **Ratio 1.0–1.5:** Consider Graduated or Extended plans if cash flow is tight.
- **Ratio > 1.5:** Income-driven plans are likely your best option, especially if you work in public service.

### Step 2: Check your Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) eligibility
If you work for a government or nonprofit, the PSLF program forgives remaining federal loan balances after 120 qualifying payments (10 years) under an IDR plan. This can make IDR plans dramatically cheaper than Standard repayment, even if your income is moderate.

### Step 3: Simulate your future income
Use QFINHUB’s **Budget Calculator** at qfinhub.com/calculators/budget to project your monthly cash flow over the next 5–10 years. Factor in career advancement, marriage, and major expenses.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Choosing the lowest monthly payment without considering total cost.** Lower payments mean more years of interest accrual. Use QFINHUB’s **Debt Payoff Calculator** at qfinhub.com/calculators/credit-card-payoff (adaptable for student loans) to see how extra payments accelerate payoff.

**Mistake 2: Ignoring tax implications of forgiveness.** Under IDR plans (except PSLF), the forgiven balance after 20–25 years is treated as taxable income. If you have $50,000 forgiven, you could owe $10,000–$15,000 in taxes that year. Plan ahead by saving in a high-yield account.

**Mistake 3: Consolidating loans without understanding the trade-offs.** Federal consolidation can simplify payments but may reset your progress toward IDR forgiveness and eliminate certain borrower benefits. Always check with your servicer first.

**Mistake 4: Not recertifying your income annually for IDR plans.** If you miss the deadline, your payment jumps to the Standard plan amount, and unpaid interest capitalizes. Set calendar reminders 60 days before your recertification date.
