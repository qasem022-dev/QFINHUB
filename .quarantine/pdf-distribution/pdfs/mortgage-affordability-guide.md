## What Is Mortgage Affordability?

Mortgage affordability isn't just about the price tag on a home. It's about understanding how a mortgage payment fits into your overall financial picture. Lenders look at your income, debts, credit score, and down payment to determine how much they're willing to lend you. But your personal comfort level may be different from what a bank approves.

**Key factors that affect affordability:**
- **Gross monthly income** – Your total income before taxes
- **Debt-to-income ratio (DTI)** – How much of your income goes to debt payments
- **Down payment** – The larger your down payment, the lower your monthly payment
- **Interest rate** – Even a 0.5% difference can cost thousands over the life of a loan
- **Property taxes and insurance** – Often escrowed into your monthly payment
- **HOA fees and maintenance** – Ongoing costs many first-time buyers overlook

## The 28/36 Rule: Your Affordability Framework

Lenders commonly use the **28/36 rule** to evaluate mortgage applications. It's a simple but powerful guideline:

| Rule Component | What It Means | Maximum Percentage |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Front-end ratio** | Total housing costs (mortgage principal + interest + taxes + insurance) | 28% of gross monthly income |
| **Back-end ratio** | All monthly debt payments (housing + car loans + student loans + credit cards) | 36% of gross monthly income |

**Example:** If your gross monthly income is $6,000:
- Maximum housing payment: $6,000 × 28% = **$1,680**
- Maximum total debt payments: $6,000 × 36% = **$2,160**

Some lenders may allow higher ratios (up to 43% for certain loan programs), but sticking to 28/36 gives you breathing room for savings, emergencies, and lifestyle expenses.

### Why This Matters for You

Even if a lender approves you for a higher amount, consider keeping your payment below the 28% threshold. A smaller mortgage means more money for retirement contributions, travel, or simply peace of mind.

## Hidden Costs That Impact Affordability

Many first-time buyers focus only on the monthly mortgage payment, but several other expenses can strain your budget. Here's a breakdown of what you should plan for:

| Cost Category | Typical Amount | Notes |
|---------------|----------------|-------|
| **Down payment** | 3–20% of home price | 20% avoids PMI; 3% possible with FHA loans |
| **Closing costs** | 2–5% of loan amount | Includes appraisal, title insurance, origination fees |
| **Property taxes** | 0.5–2.5% of home value annually | Varies by location; check local rates |
| **Homeowners insurance** | $800–$1,500/year | Required by lenders; flood insurance may be extra |
| **PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance)** | 0.5–1% of loan amount annually | Required if down payment < 20% |
| **Maintenance & repairs** | 1–2% of home value annually | Save for roof, HVAC, plumbing, etc. |
| **HOA fees** | $100–$500/month | Common in condos and planned communities |

**Real-world example:** On a $300,000 home with 10% down and a 6.5% interest rate, your monthly payment might look like this:
- Principal & interest: ~$1,700
- Property taxes: ~$250
- Insurance: ~$100
- PMI: ~$125
- **Total: ~$2,175/month** (not including utilities or maintenance)

## How Your Credit Score Affects Affordability

Your credit score directly impacts the interest rate you qualify for—and even a small difference can change what you can afford.

| Credit Score Range | Typical Interest Rate (30-year fixed) | Monthly Payment on $300k Loan | Total Interest Paid |
|-------------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| 760+ | 6.25% | $1,847 | $364,920 |
| 700–759 | 6.75% | $1,946 | $400,560 |
| 640–699 | 7.50% | $2,097 | $454,920 |
| Below 640 | 8.50%+ | $2,307 | $530,520 |

**Key takeaway:** Improving your credit score by 60 points could save you over **$90,000** in interest over 30 years.

### Steps to Improve Your Credit Before Buying
1. Check your credit report for errors (free at AnnualCreditReport.com)
2. Pay all bills on time—even one late payment can drop your score
3. Keep credit card balances below 30% of your credit limit
4. Avoid opening new credit accounts 6–12 months before applying for a mortgage

## Renting vs. Buying: Which Makes More Sense for You?

Mortgage affordability isn't just about whether you can buy—it's about whether you *should* buy. Here's a quick comparison:

| Factor | Renting | Buying |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Monthly payment | Fixed for lease term | Can increase with taxes/insurance |
| Upfront cost | Security deposit (1–2 months rent) | 3–20% down + closing costs |
| Equity building | None | Builds over time |
| Maintenance | Landlord's responsibility | Yours |
| Flexibility | Easy to move | Selling takes time and money |

**The 5-year rule:** Generally, buying makes more financial sense if you plan to stay in the home for at least 5 years. This gives you time to recoup closing costs and benefit from appreciation.

## Putting It All Together: Your Affordability Action Plan

Ready to determine your true mortgage affordability? Follow these steps:

1. **Calculate your gross monthly income** – Include salary, bonuses, side hustles
2. **List all monthly debt payments** – Car loans, student loans, credit card minimums
3. **Apply the 28/36 rule** – Determine your maximum housing payment
4. **Factor in hidden costs** – Taxes, insurance, PMI, maintenance
5. **Check your credit score** – Know where you stand before shopping for rates
6. **Save for a down payment** – Aim for 20% if possible, but don't let perfect be the enemy of good
7. **Get pre-approved** – This shows sellers you're serious and locks in your rate
8. **Use QFINHUB calculators** – Run multiple scenarios to find your sweet spot

### A Word of Caution

Don't let a lender's maximum approval amount be your target. Just because you *can* borrow $400,000 doesn't mean you *should*. A house that stretches your budget can leave you "house poor"—with a beautiful home but no money for vacations, retirement, or emergencies.

**Aim for a mortgage payment that leaves you with at least 20% of your income for savings and discretionary spending.**
