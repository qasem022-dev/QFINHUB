What Is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest earned on both your original principal and the interest that accumulates over time. Unlike simple interest, which only pays on your initial amount, compound interest creates a snowball effect: your money earns money, and that new money earns even more money.

The formula for compound interest:

\[ A = P \times (1 + \frac{r}{n})^{n \times t} \]

Where:

  • A = final amount
  • P = initial principal
  • r = annual interest rate (decimal)
  • n = number of times interest compounds per year
  • t = number of years

    Real-World Example

    Suppose you invest $10,000 at 8% annual interest. After 30 years:

  • Simple interest: $10,000 + ($10,000 × 0.08 × 30) = $34,000
  • Compound interest (monthly): $10,000 × (1 + 0.08/12)^(12×30) ≈ $109,357

    The compound interest version earns over 3x more—without you lifting a finger.

    | Year | Simple Interest Balance | Compound Interest Balance | |------|------------------------|--------------------------| | 0 | $10,000 | $10,000 | | 10 | $18,000 | $22,196 | | 20 | $26,000 | $49,268 | | 30 | $34,000 | $109,357 |

    Data: 8% annual rate, monthly compounding.

    The gap widens dramatically over time. This is why starting early matters more than starting large.

    The Three Levers of Compound Interest

    You control three variables that determine your compound growth:

    1. Time (Your Biggest Advantage) Every year you delay investing, you lose not just that year's growth—you lose the growth on that growth. A 25-year-old who invests $5,000 annually until age 35 (10 years total) will likely have more at retirement than a 35-year-old who invests $5,000 annually for 30 years. That's the power of an extra decade of compounding.

    Actionable tip: Use QFINHUB's Compound Interest Calculator to see how starting 5 years earlier changes your outcome. The difference will shock you.

    2. Rate of Return A 2% difference in annual return can double your final balance over 30 years. This doesn't mean chasing risky investments—it means being disciplined about fees, asset allocation, and tax efficiency.

    Actionable tip: Compare scenarios at 6%, 8%, and 10% returns using the same calculator. You'll see why low-fee index funds often beat actively managed funds over long periods.

    3. Frequency of Compounding Daily compounding beats monthly, which beats annual. Most savings accounts compound daily; most investments compound when dividends are reinvested. Always choose accounts that compound more frequently.

    Quick comparison on $10,000 at 8% for 30 years:

    | Compounding Frequency | Final Balance | |----------------------|---------------| | Annually | $100,627 | | Quarterly | $107,322 | | Monthly | $109,357 | | Daily | $110,130 |

    The difference is meaningful, but time and rate matter far more.

    The Rule of 72: A Quick Mental Shortcut

    Want to know how long it takes your money to double? Divide 72 by your annual interest rate.

  • At 6%: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years to double
  • At 8%: 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years
  • At 10%: 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years

    This rule works for inflation too. At 3% inflation, your money's purchasing power halves in 72 ÷ 3 = 24 years.
