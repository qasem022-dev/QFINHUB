# Home Buying Checklist: From Pre-Approval to Closing

This comprehensive checklist breaks down every major step—from getting pre-approved to signing the final documents—so you can navigate the journey with confidence. ## 1. Before You Start: Assess Your Financial Health

Before you even tour a home, you need a clear picture of your finances. Lenders will scrutinize your income, debts, credit score, and savings. Start here:

### Check Your Credit Score
Your credit score directly impacts your mortgage interest rate. A higher score can save you tens of thousands of dollars over the life of a loan. Aim for a score of 740 or higher for the best rates. You can check your score for free through most credit card issuers or annualcreditreport.com.

### Calculate Your Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)
Lenders prefer a DTI below 43% (including your future mortgage payment). Use the **QFINHUB Budget Calculator** (qfinhub.com/calculators/budget) to track your monthly income and existing debt payments. For example:

| Monthly Income | Existing Debt Payments | Max Mortgage Payment (at 43% DTI) |
|----------------|------------------------|------------------------------------|
| $6,000         | $800                   | $1,780 (43% of $6,000 = $2,580 – $800) |

### Save for a Down Payment and Closing Costs
A 20% down payment avoids private mortgage insurance (PMI), but many loans accept as little as 3–5%. Closing costs typically add 2–5% of the purchase price. Use the **QFINHUB Savings Goal Calculator** (qfinhub.com/calculators/savings-goal) to set a timeline for your down payment fund.

## 2. Step One: Get Pre-Approved

A pre-approval letter shows sellers you’re a serious buyer and locks in your budget. This is different from pre-qualification (which is less formal).

### What You’ll Need:
- Recent pay stubs (2 months)
- W-2s or tax returns (2 years)
- Bank statements (2–3 months)
- Photo ID

### How Much Can You Afford?
Don’t rely solely on a lender’s maximum—they may approve you for more than you can comfortably pay. ## 3. Step Two: Find the Right Home and Make an Offer

Once you’re pre-approved, start house hunting with a clear budget. Work with a trusted real estate agent who knows your target neighborhoods.

### Key Considerations:
- **Location:** Schools, commute times, and neighborhood safety
- **Condition:** Older homes may need costly repairs—budget for a home inspection
- **Future Resale Value:** Avoid over-improving for the area

### Making an Offer
Your agent will help you craft a competitive offer based on comparable sales (comps). Include contingencies for financing, inspection, and appraisal to protect yourself. If the seller accepts, you’ll enter escrow.

---

## 4. Step Three: Secure Financing and Lock Your Rate

After your offer is accepted, it’s time to finalize your mortgage. You have several loan options:

- **Conventional Loan:** Requires good credit and 3–20% down
- **FHA Loan:** Lower credit score minimum (580) and 3.5% down
- **VA Loan:** No down payment for eligible veterans
- **USDA Loan:** No down payment for rural buyers

### Lock Your Interest Rate
Rates can fluctuate daily. Ask your lender about a rate lock (typically 30–60 days) to protect against increases. Use the **QFINHUB Loan Calculator** (qfinhub.com/calculators/loan) to compare monthly payments for different rates and terms.

**Example: $300,000 Loan at Different Rates**

| Interest Rate | Monthly Payment (30-year fixed) | Total Interest Paid |
|---------------|--------------------------------|---------------------|
| 6.0%          | $1,799                        | $347,514           |
| 6.5%          | $1,896                        | $382,633           |
| 7.0%          | $1,996                        | $418,527           |

A 0.5% difference can cost you over $35,000 in interest over 30 years. Always shop around with at least 3–4 lenders.

## 5. Step Four: Due Diligence – Inspections, Appraisals, and Insurance

This phase protects you from buying a money pit. Don’t skip these steps.

### Home Inspection
Hire a certified inspector to check the roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and more. If major issues arise (e.g., termite damage or a leaky roof), you can renegotiate or walk away.

### Appraisal
Your lender requires an appraisal to ensure the home is worth the purchase price. If the appraisal comes in low, you may need to increase your down payment or negotiate with the seller.

### Homeowners Insurance
Lenders require insurance before closing. Shop around for policies that cover fire, theft, liability, and natural disasters (flood insurance may be separate).

### Compare Rent vs. Buy
If you’re unsure about committing, use the **QFINHUB Rent vs Buy Calculator** (qfinhub.com/calculators/rent-vs-buy). It factors in closing costs, maintenance, and appreciation to show you the breakeven point.

## 6. Step Five: Final Walkthrough and Closing

A few days before closing, do a final walkthrough to verify the home is in the agreed-upon condition and any requested repairs are complete.

### What to Bring to Closing:
- Government-issued ID
- Certified or cashier’s check for closing costs (or wire transfer instructions)
- Proof of homeowners insurance

### Closing Costs Breakdown
Closing costs typically include:
- Loan origination fees (0.5–1% of loan)
- Title insurance and escrow fees
- Property taxes and prepaid interest
- Recording fees

Use the **QFINHUB Mortgage Affordability Calculator** to estimate total closing costs based on your loan amount.

### Signing Day
You’ll sign dozens of documents, including the promissory note (your promise to repay) and the deed of trust. Once everything is funded, you’ll receive the keys. Congratulations—you’re now a homeowner!
