## The Number You're Looking at Is Wrong

Open your 401(k) statement. See that number? Maybe it's $50,000. Maybe it's $250,000. Maybe it's higher.

That number is **not** how much money you'll have in retirement. It's not even close.

Here's why: your 401(k) balance is a snapshot in time. It doesn't account for future contributions, employer matches, compound growth, inflation erosion, taxes in retirement, or how long you'll actually live.

**The real question isn't "what's my balance?" — it's "what will my monthly income be in retirement?"** And that question requires a different kind of math entirely.

## The 5 Numbers That Actually Matter

### 1. Your True Monthly Expenses (Not Your Guess)

Most people underestimate their monthly expenses by 20-30%. Why? They forget about:
- Annual insurance premiums ($2,000-5,000/year)
- Home maintenance (1-2% of home value/year)
- Car replacement fund ($200-400/month)
- Healthcare costs (average $7,000-12,000/year before Medicare)
- Travel and gifts ($2,000-5,000/year)

**Try this:** Track every expense for 30 days using our [free budget planner](/calculators/budget-planner). Multiply by 12. Now add 15% for irregular expenses. *That's* your real monthly spend.

### 2. Your Retirement Income Gap

Social Security replaces about 40% of pre-retirement income for the average earner. If you need $5,000/month and Social Security provides $2,000, you have a **$3,000/month gap** that your savings must fill.

Over a 25-year retirement, that's $900,000 in today's dollars. With 3% annual inflation, it's closer to $1.4 million.

### 3. The Safe Withdrawal Rate (It's Not Always 4%)

The "4% rule" says you can withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one, then adjust for inflation. But this rule was designed for 30-year retirements starting at 65.

If you're planning to retire at 55 (a 35-40 year retirement), the safe rate drops to 3.0-3.5%. For early retirees at 45, it may be 2.5-2.8%.

**The math:** To generate $36,000/year ($3,000/month gap):
- At 4% withdrawal: You need $900,000 saved
- At 3.5% withdrawal: You need $1,028,000 saved
- At 3% withdrawal: You need $1,200,000 saved

That's a $300,000 difference — based entirely on when you retire.

### 4. The Hidden Tax Bracket in Retirement

Your 401(k) contributions are pre-tax, which means **every dollar you withdraw in retirement is taxed as ordinary income.** 

If you withdraw $60,000/year from a traditional 401(k) in 2026, you'll pay roughly $6,000-8,000 in federal taxes (effective rate 10-13%). But if tax rates rise — and many economists predict they will — you could pay significantly more.

**Roth vs Traditional:** Roth accounts are funded with after-tax dollars but grow tax-free. For younger earners in lower tax brackets, Roth contributions often beat traditional by $50,000-200,000 over a career.

### 5. Compound Interest: Your Best Friend (or Worst Enemy)

Here's the most important number in retirement planning:

**Starting early is worth more than doubling your contributions.**

| Start Age | Monthly Contribution | Balance at 65 (7% return) |
|:---:|:---:|---:|
| 25 | $500 | $1,198,000 |
| 35 | $500 | $566,000 |
| 45 | $500 | $246,000 |
| 25 | $250 | $599,000 |
| 35 | $1,000 | $1,132,000 |

Starting at 25 with $500/month beats starting at 35 with $1,000/month. **That's the power of 10 extra years of compounding.** Run your own numbers with our [compound interest calculator](/calculators/compound-interest).

## The Actual Formula (Simplified)

Your retirement readiness = (Total Savings × Safe Withdrawal Rate) + Social Security + Other Income − Monthly Expenses

If the result is positive, you're on track. If negative, you need to adjust one of these variables:
1. Save more (increase contribution rate)
2. Work longer (more compounding years)
3. Reduce expenses (lower your target number)
4. Increase returns (optimize your portfolio)

## Real Example: Sarah, 35, $80,000 Income

Sarah has $75,000 in her 401(k), contributes 10% ($8,000/year), and gets a 4% employer match ($3,200/year). She wants to retire at 62.

**Without running the numbers, Sarah thinks she's doing fine.**

**Running the numbers with our [retirement planning calculator](/calculators/retirement-planning):**
- Current savings: $75,000
- Annual contributions: $11,200 (including match)
- Years to retirement: 27
- Expected market return: 7%
- **Projected balance at 62: $988,000**

At 4% withdrawal: $39,520/year ($3,293/month)
Social Security estimate: $2,400/month
**Total retirement income: $5,693/month (pre-tax)**

If Sarah's expenses are $5,000/month, she's in good shape. If they're $6,500/month, she has a $807/month shortfall — and needs to save an additional $400/month starting now.

Your 401(k) balance is the starting point, not the answer. The real question is whether your projected retirement income covers your projected retirement expenses.
