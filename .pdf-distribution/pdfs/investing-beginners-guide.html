Section 2: Choose the Right Account Type

Your $100 needs a home. Here are the best account options for beginners:

Taxable Brokerage Account
  • No contribution limits
  • Withdraw anytime (taxes apply on gains)
  • Best for short-term goals (1–5 years)

    Roth IRA (Individual Retirement Account)
  • Tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement
  • 2025 contribution limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+)
  • Early withdrawal penalty applies before age 59½
  • Best for long-term goals (5+ years)

    Recommendation: If you can commit to leaving the money invested until retirement, open a Roth IRA. Otherwise, use a standard brokerage account.

    Where to Open an Account Popular beginner-friendly platforms: Fidelity, Charles Schwab, Vanguard, Robinhood, or M1 Finance. All allow fractional shares, so your $100 can buy portions of expensive stocks like Amazon or Google.

    Section 3: What to Buy with $100

    With $100, diversification is key. You don't want all your money in one stock. Here are three proven strategies:

    Option A: A Single Broad-Market ETF (Recommended) Buy one share of a low-cost ETF like:
  • VTI (Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF) – ~$260 per share? Use fractional shares.
  • SPLG (SPDR Portfolio S&P 500 ETF) – ~$65 per share, you can buy 1.5 shares.
  • VT (Vanguard Total World Stock ETF) – ~$115 per share.

    These funds hold thousands of companies, spreading risk automatically.

    Option B: Build a Two-Fund Portfolio Split your $100:
  • 80% in a total stock market ETF (VTI or SPLG)
  • 20% in a bond or international ETF (BND or VXUS)

    Option C: A Single Dividend Stock If you prefer individual stocks, consider a stable dividend payer like Coca-Cola (KO) or Johnson & Johnson (JNJ). You'll receive small dividend payments quarterly.

    | Investment Option | Risk Level | Potential Return | Minimum Investment | |------------------|------------|------------------|-------------------| | S&P 500 ETF (SPLG) | Medium | 7–10% annually | ~$65 for 1 share | | Total Market ETF (VT) | Medium | 7–9% annually | ~$115 for 1 share | | Dividend Stock (KO) | Medium-Low | 5–8% annually | ~$60 for 1 share |

    Section 4: The Power of Compound Interest

    Your $100 won't make you rich overnight, but compound interest turns small amounts into significant wealth over decades.

    How Compounding Works
  • You invest $100 and earn 8% in year one → $108
  • Year two: earn 8% on $108 → $116.64
  • Year three: earn 8% on $116.64 → $125.97

    Each year, you earn returns on your original money plus previous returns.

    Section 5: Dollar-Cost Averaging vs. Lump Sum

    You have $100 today. Should you invest it all at once or spread it out?

    Lump Sum Investing
  • Invest the full $100 immediately
  • Historically outperforms in 2 out of 3 years
  • Best if market is not at all-time highs

    Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)
  • Invest $25 per week for 4 weeks
  • Reduces risk of buying at a peak
  • Good for nervous beginners

    Our advice: Invest the full $100 now. Time in the market beats timing the market. Set up automatic recurring investments of $20–$50 per month going forward.
